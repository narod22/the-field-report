🌍 World Bank  ·  Policy + Governance
The WDR 2026 is here. The headline is cautious optimism. The warning is more important.

The World Bank released its World Development Report 2026, focused entirely on AI for development. The headline finding: AI-delivered business advice via smartphones is already producing measurable productivity gains for small enterprises in several markets. That's the good news. The warning buried in Chapter 3 is sharper — computing infrastructure gaps, data deficits, and skills shortages could widen the divide between high and lower-income countries rather than close it. For consultants working on digital transformation projects, this document is now your go-to reference for grounding theory-of-change conversations.

Skip to Chapter 3 if you're short on time. It's the most practical section for consultants advising on AI readiness.

🤖 DeepSeek  ·  General AI, Development Lens
DeepSeek V4 cuts prices 700x vs. GPT. For development organizations, the infrastructure angle matters more than the benchmarks.

DeepSeek released V4 this week with dramatically lower API pricing and, critically, support for Huawei chips. Most coverage focused on the US-China AI race angle. The more useful lens for this audience: development organizations operating in Africa and Asia often face restricted access to US cloud infrastructure — AWS and Azure are either unavailable, prohibitively expensive, or politically complicated. Lower-cost AI that runs on alternative hardware changes the deployment calculus for orgs trying to build internal AI capacity in constrained contexts.

If you're advising on AI strategy for a country office with infrastructure constraints, this story belongs in your next briefing.

📋 OpenAI  ·  Accountability Watch
Sam Altman published OpenAI's principles. Development professionals should read them as accountability commitments, not marketing.

Altman published five principles: Democratization, Empowerment, Universal Prosperity, Resilience, and Adaptability. The framing is notable — AI power should be distributed broadly, not captured by a handful of companies, and democratic processes should shape how this technology develops. For development sector professionals, the interesting question is how these principles hold when you notice they were published the same day a $134B governance trial began. Read the principles first. Then read the timeline. That gap is where the accountability conversation starts.

🔬 Anthropic Research  ·  Model Performance
Anthropic's AI agents negotiated real deals in a test marketplace. Weaker models lost without knowing it. The quality gap is consequential.

Anthropic ran "Project Deal," a test environment where AI agents representing buyers and sellers negotiated real agreements. Strong models reached favorable outcomes. Weaker models consistently lost — and had no awareness that they were losing. For development organizations deploying AI agents on high-stakes tasks (procurement, proposal matching, evaluation scoring), this is a useful data point: the performance gap between frontier models and cheaper alternatives is real, measurable, and consequential. Not all AI is equal for work that matters.

Skill of the week
The 5 inputs that fix generic AI output — before you write a single prompt

The most common frustration with AI in consulting work: you ask for something specific and get something generic. You clean it up for an hour and decide it wasn't worth it. This is almost always a briefing problem, not an AI capability problem. Before asking AI for anything serious — a report section, a proposal draft, an analysis — give it these five things.

  1. 01
    Who you are. Your role, your organization type, the sector you work in, the context of this project. "I am an independent consultant working on a UNICEF capacity assessment in West Africa" is infinitely more useful than "write me a report."
  2. 02
    The role of AI in this task. Are you asking it to act as a junior analyst, a copy editor, a critical reviewer, a research assistant? Naming the role shapes how it responds.
  3. 03
    Project details and deliverables. What is this project? What are the deliverables? What's the audience? A field assessment for a country director reads differently than one for a donor.
  4. 04
    Objectives and success criteria. What does a good output look like? What does it need to accomplish? What would make you send it back immediately?
  5. 05
    An example of excellent past work. Upload a report section or proposal you're proud of. Tell it: "This is the quality and style I expect." AI calibrates fast when it has a real benchmark.

Paste these five things into a Claude Project as your system instructions and every conversation in that project starts with full context — no re-explaining required. Here's a starter template:

Copy-paste starter template
I am [your role] working in [sector/geography]. My typical clients are [type of organization]. My deliverables include [reports / evaluations / proposals / other].

In this project, you are acting as my AI co-consultant — a highly capable junior analyst who follows instructions precisely, asks clarifying questions when something is ambiguous, and never fabricates data or sources.

The audience for most of my work is [audience]. The tone should be [professional/technical/accessible]. My quality standard is attached — match the structure and depth of that example.

When I give you feedback, apply it specifically and immediately. If something is unclear, ask before proceeding.

Data privacy note: System instructions in Claude Projects are not shared across sessions and are not visible to other users. Never paste beneficiary names, case numbers, or personally identifiable information. Summarize sensitive data before input — the summary goes in, the raw data stays local.
