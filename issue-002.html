🏕 WFP  ·  AI in the Field
WFP launched HungerMap Live. AI-assisted hunger forecasting is in production in 50+ countries right now.

On April 16, WFP released HungerMap Live — a rebuilt intelligence platform integrating food security monitoring from 300+ analysts across 50+ countries with AI-assisted forecasting backed by Google.org. This is not a pilot. It is running now, and the data is publicly accessible.

  • The platform answers three questions every humanitarian planner needs: what is the current food security state, which countries need urgent attention right now, and what is driving the crisis.
  • WFP's anticipatory action programs return $7 for every $1 invested — earlier crisis signals are the mechanism that makes that return possible.
  • HungerMap pulls data from satellite imagery, market price feeds, conflict indicators, and 300+ analyst networks into a single dashboard updated continuously.
  • The live platform is publicly accessible at hungermap.wfp.org — useful as a reference tool even if you're not working directly in food security.
For ID consultants: For consultants designing monitoring frameworks or theory-of-change documents that reference early warning systems, HungerMap Live is now a concrete, citable example of AI in production at scale. It's also a useful benchmark for what "AI-assisted decision-making" looks like when it's actually working — not a pilot, not a proof of concept.

📊 Stanford HAI  ·  Policy + Governance
Stanford's 2026 AI Index: only 44 countries have state-backed AI compute. Most client countries aren't on that list.

Published April 13, the Stanford AI Index finds that state-backed supercomputing infrastructure is concentrated in 44 nations — and explicitly warns that South American, Central Asian, and Sub-Saharan African countries lag significantly, risking a new digital divide in exactly the geographies where development work concentrates. AI engineering skills are growing fastest in UAE, Chile, and South Africa, but infrastructure tells a different story.

For ID consultants: If you're working on a digital strategy or AI readiness assessment for a government client, the AI Index is the annual benchmark worth citing. The sovereign compute gap is the structural problem framing most development-sector AI conversations right now.

🤖 DeepSeek  ·  General AI, Development Lens
DeepSeek V4 costs $3.48 per million tokens. OpenAI charges $30. For constrained-budget organizations, this is a real number.

DeepSeek released V4 under the MIT license — meaning it can be downloaded, run locally, and modified without ongoing API costs. V4 also runs on Huawei chips, not Nvidia, which matters for organizations operating in countries where US cloud infrastructure is restricted, expensive, or politically complicated. The economics of local AI deployment just shifted.

For ID consultants: The MIT license is the operative detail — government agencies and NGOs can deploy this locally without API fees. Worth a line in your next feasibility analysis if you're working in a constrained infrastructure context.

📋 IRC  ·  Accountability Watch
IRC's honest look at AI ambition vs. funding cuts: the pilots are stalled, and the money is gone.

The International Rescue Committee published a direct look at the tension defining humanitarian AI in 2026: organizations are being pushed to do more with less at the same moment they were supposed to be scaling AI programs built during the boom years. AI pilots are stalled not because the technology failed, but because the organizations running them ran out of money — USAID cuts eliminated roughly 83% of grants in 2025 alone.

For ID consultants: The pain point organizations are solving for right now isn't "find us new AI tools" — it's "help us do the work we were doing, faster, with fewer people." That's a positioning opportunity for consultants who can deliver on it.

This week's skill
How to build a 30-page consulting report with AI — in days, not weeks

One of the most-requested topics from the AI-Enabled Consultant workshop was report writing: how do you produce a well-structured, evidence-grounded report from a pile of transcripts and data files without spending weeks on it? Here's the exact three-step framework from the workshop, pulled directly from a real UNICEF Chad project — 10 hours of multilingual field transcripts, four years of transaction data, and a 30-page report produced in days.

  1. 01
    Prepare the project. Create a Claude Project (or any AI system that maintains persistent context). Write a system prompt covering: what is this project, who is the audience, what is the deliverable, what your role is as the consultant. Then upload your source documents — interview transcripts, previous reports, data tables, reference frameworks. The AI now has everything it needs to work from your material, not from general knowledge. This setup takes 20-30 minutes and saves days.
  2. 02
    Build the outline first — and push back on it. Ask for a three-level-deep outline before you write a word of the report. Don't accept the first version. Iterate 2-3 times: Does every major section connect to an evaluation question? Is the balance between sections right? Are there gaps or overlaps? The outline governs everything that follows — this is the highest-leverage 30 minutes in the entire process.
  3. 03
    Draft section by section, and make the AI audit itself. Take your approved outline and work through it one section at a time. After each draft, add one validation step: "Does this section accurately reflect the uploaded source documents? Flag any claim or finding that isn't directly supported by the data you have." The AI audits its own output against what you gave it — this is what prevents hallucinated findings from making it into your deliverable.

Starter prompt for Step 2 — adapt this to your project:

Copy-paste starter prompt
"I'm building a [report type, e.g. program evaluation] for [client/context]. My evaluation questions are: [list 3-5]. I've uploaded [source documents]. Draft a three-level-deep outline. Every major section should map to at least one evaluation question. For each section, include a brief note on the primary sources you'll draw from."

This workflow keeps sensitive source material inside your Claude Project — not shared, not used for model training (with Pro or API settings), and not sent to third-party platforms. Summarize any personally identifiable data before uploading. The raw transcripts stay local; the summaries go into the project.
