🏕 WFP  ·  AI in the Field
WFP launched HungerMap Live. It uses AI-assisted forecasting to spot hunger crises before they spiral — and it's live right now in 50+ countries.

On April 16, WFP released HungerMap Live, a rebuilt intelligence platform that integrates food security monitoring data from 300+ analysts across 50+ countries with AI-assisted forecasting backed by Google.org. The platform answers three specific questions every humanitarian planner needs: what is the current state of food security, which countries need urgent attention right now, and what are the drivers. The operational payoff is concrete — WFP's anticipatory action programs return $7 for every $1 invested, and earlier crisis signals are the mechanism that makes that possible. This is not a pilot. It's running.

For consultants designing monitoring frameworks or theory-of-change documents that reference early warning systems, HungerMap Live is now a concrete, citable example of AI in production at scale. Visit hungermap.wfp.org to see the live data.

📊 Stanford HAI  ·  Policy + Governance
Stanford's 2026 AI Index: only 44 countries have state-backed AI compute. Most of your client countries are not on that list.

The Stanford Human-Centered AI Institute published its annual AI Index on April 13. The finding that matters most for this audience: the number of nations with state-backed supercomputing clusters sits at 44 — and the report explicitly warns that South American and Middle Eastern countries lag significantly, risking a new "digital divide" where nations that can't shape AI development are less likely to see its economic benefits. Meanwhile, AI engineering skills are growing fastest in UAE, Chile, and South Africa. The picture is uneven, and the gap between adoption and actual capacity-building is widening in exactly the geographies where development work concentrates.

If you're working on a digital strategy or AI readiness assessment for a government client, the Stanford AI Index is now an annual benchmark worth citing. The sovereign AI infrastructure gap is the single most important structural challenge for the sector.

🤖 DeepSeek  ·  General AI, Development Lens
DeepSeek V4 costs $3.48 per million output tokens. OpenAI charges $30. For development organizations with constrained budgets, this is a real number.

DeepSeek released a preview of its V4 model on April 24 under the MIT license — meaning it can be downloaded, run locally, and modified. The pricing: $3.48 per million output tokens, compared to $30 for OpenAI's comparable offering. More important for this audience than the benchmark numbers: V4 is designed to run on Huawei chips, not Nvidia. Development organizations operating in countries where US cloud infrastructure is restricted, expensive, or politically complicated now have a frontier-capable model that doesn't require AWS or Azure. That changes the deployment math for orgs trying to build internal AI capacity in Africa, Central Asia, and Southeast Asia.

The open-source, MIT-licensed nature means government agencies and NGOs can deploy this without ongoing API costs. For consultants advising on AI strategy, this is worth a line in your feasibility analysis.

📋 IRC  ·  Accountability Watch
Innovation vs. Cuts: the IRC's honest accounting of what happens when AI ambition meets a sector losing its funding floor.

The International Rescue Committee published a direct look at the tension that defines humanitarian AI in 2026: organizations are being pushed to do more with less at the same moment they were supposed to be scaling AI programs built during the boom years. USAID cuts eliminated roughly 83% of grants in 2025, and global humanitarian funding continues to contract. The result: AI pilots are stalled, not because the technology failed, but because the organizations running them ran out of money. The sector built ambitious AI programs when the funding was there. The funding is gone and the tools are sitting idle. For independent consultants, the opportunity embedded in this story is real — organizations need help doing more with constrained resources, and that is precisely what AI-enabled workflows can provide.

This piece is worth reading if you're positioning yourself for consulting work with humanitarian organizations. The pain point they're solving for is not "find us new AI tools" — it's "help us do the work we were doing, faster, with fewer people."

This week's skill
Turn 20 KII transcripts into a usable data matrix in under an hour

Key informant interviews generate the most useful data in any evaluation — and the most labor-intensive synthesis work. Most consultants spend 2-3 days manually coding transcripts. Here's a workflow that cuts that to under an hour, without sacrificing rigor.

  • 01
    Build your codebook first. Before touching transcripts, give AI your evaluation questions and ask it to generate a codebook: 8-12 themes with definitions, sub-codes, and example quotes. Review and edit. This takes 15 minutes and makes everything else faster.
  • 02
    Process transcripts in batches. Paste 3-5 transcripts at a time with a clear instruction: "Code these transcripts using the attached codebook. For each theme, extract the most relevant quotes and note which respondent said them." Never paste all transcripts in one message — you lose precision.
  • 03
    Build the matrix separately. Once all transcripts are coded, ask AI to organize findings into a data matrix: themes as rows, respondent categories as columns, with quote counts and key examples. This is the table that goes in your annex and feeds your analysis section.
  • 04
    Flag divergence explicitly. Add one instruction: "Note any themes where respondents from different groups gave significantly different responses." This surfaces the analytical tension points you'll want to address — the places where the data doesn't agree.
Copy-paste prompt
"I'm conducting a [type] evaluation in [sector/geography]. My key evaluation questions are: [list 3-5 questions]. Generate a codebook with 8-10 themes, each with: a definition (2 sentences), 2-3 sub-codes, and an example of what a relevant quote would look like. Format as a table."

This workflow keeps your transcripts in a single session and doesn't require uploading files to third-party platforms. Works with Claude Projects, ChatGPT, or Gemini — all can hold a codebook in context while you process individual transcripts.
